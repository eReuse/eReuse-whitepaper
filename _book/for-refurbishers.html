We need to know what works, what doesn’t and why and that means turning to metrics. The field of metrics can be difficult to navigate due to the variety of terms and definitions used. What is the difference between a metric and an indicator, and is a standard or a certificate more suitable for your needs? Further on the document we present the various terms defined by Verstraeten-Jochemsen, Baars, and Daniels (2020) and in the credentials section we will detail the metrics we consider necessary to measure the performance of refurbishers.

6.1.1 Metric

A metric is a method we employ to understand change over time across a number of dimensions. We use it as a catch-all term to describe the method used to measure something, the resulting values, as well as a calculated or combined set of indicators.

6.1.2 Indicator

The indicator is a crucial element of a metric, referring to a single value and its unit, and used to indicate (hence the name) a specific trend or performance.

6.1.3 Tool

Many of the metrics considered are partly or fully automated in online or offline tools that enable easy application and reduce the risk of errors. In the context of this publication, tools can therefore be considered automated metrics.

6.1.4 Standard

Metrics that have gone a certain degree of scrutiny and testing, and are commonly accepted as a standardised way of measuring, can be regarded as standards. Technically, one can consider both ‘informal’ standards and standards that have officially been documented and published by standardisation organizations but in the context of this publication, we will only use the term to refer to the latter.

6.1.5 Certificate

A certificate is a specific type of standard that can not be applied by the organisation itself but requires a third party to assess whether the company’s performance has been according to a certain standard. They usually result in a product label being issued by the third party.

6.2 Verify

There are certain requirements that the refurbishers’ system must have in order to allow for this verification. The requirements are inmutability, verificability and privacy of the data.

  • Immutability means unchangeability. Immutable data is a piece of information in a database that cannot be (or shouldn’t be) deleted or modified after it’s creation. Most traditional databases store data in a mutable format, meaning the database overwrites the older data when new data is available. This is where Distributed Ledger Technologies (DLTs) or blockchains come into play. Immutability is one of the key features of DLTs. It is a replicated and synchronised digital registry, where it is written by consensus of the participants, distributed to different locations and organisations, without a central administrator, which structures the data and operations in such a way that only data can be added but it is not possible to modify those already accepted. It is impossible for any entity (for example, a government or corporation) to manipulate,replace, or falsify data stored on a DLT.

  • Verifiability is an action to be able to confirm that a fact/data is supported by an attestation at a certain moment.

In this verification that is only possible with certain data, but without the data, only with a summary/signature). In short, the function that a notary or notary public performs.

6.3 Privacy

Discarded devices by public or private administrations (Product Owners) are collected and refurbished or recycled if it has been detected that there is no demand. When the Product Owner requires impact data such as lifetime extension they request a report from the refurbisher. This impact data in turn feeds into the product owner metrics to calculate their circularity performance.

In the process of data collection, the refurbisher must request data from his customers and suppliers, and in turn, to them request to the following actors of the reverse supply chain until the final recycler.

This data if its not protected could disclose the actors business privacy, but if its not accountable that do no allow to account the circular impact generatet such as extended life time and final recycling.

In order to know the impact, the refurbisher needs to ask its partners and customers the device chain-of-custody and the impact data. In cases where the refurbisher has made the sale or donation it may know if the device has been reused, but there are situations that it does not know who is the Final User. As in the case of reuse, the refurbisher also does not know if the Final User has taken it to a recycling point the device after its usage. Similarly in the case of recycling, the refurbisher when referring non-reusable devices to a recycler does not know if the devices have been recycled.
