Chapter 5 Concepts and Challenges

What eReuse federated members have in common is that we share a definition of circular economy and metrics to measure our transition to a circular economy. Together we identified the challenges to be overcome and defined the technology roadmap that will help us in this process.

5.1 What is the reuse of electronics?

Figure 5.1: Citizen and professional reuse of digital devices

The reuse of electronic devices such as desktops, laptops or mobile phones is applied to devices that have already been manufactured and are no longer in use (disposed) and will be recycled unless they are “disposed for reuse” by doing any of the following actions refurbish, recondition, repair, upgrade and used again by the same final user or redistributed to other users.

We say a device or component is reusable if it has or may have use value for someone:

  • If the use value of the device is high enough, it means that there is somewhere a potential user for that device as it is, and only a basic refurbishing processes is required, such as basic repair, erasing data and restoring the operating system (this is represented as citizen reuse loop in figure 5.1.

  • If the use value is too low, its use value can be increased through advanced refurbishing processes: repairing, replacing damaged components and updating/ugrading. This is represented as professional reuse loop in the figure.

The reuse process ends when after a few years the device or component reaches disposal for recyling state, which means its use value then, or through potential improvements, does not allow its reuse again. The cycle reach recycling to recover (reuse) raw materials and manufacture new components.

5.2 What is the Circular Economy of Electronics?

The circular economy is the vision of a new economy that aims to meet society’s needs within the limits of negative impact in order to have a healthy planet with sufficient resources for future generations. Today, we are far from achieving this vision, in 2020 our economy will be only 9% circular (Circle Economy, 2019).

The circular economy is a new paradigm to change the current consumption model based on buy, use and throw away. The purpose of the circular economy in electronics is, first, to preserve in use the products and components that have been already manufactured, and second, to recover raw materials when devices are no longer repairable and functional.

Circularity means making self-sufficient system that does not require inputs of raw materials and does not have losses. Losses are: raw materials that ends up mixed in iron or steel (because perform a basic recycling process), non-potentially recoverable materials due to the chemical characteristics of the elements - the entropy backfire, because its highest Termodynamic Rarity1 or waste that ends up in landfills.

In circular economy, products and materials are seen as assets, businesses are seen as its users, that should repair, upgrade and reuse their devices and materials to maintain them in use as much as possible.

To make and scale a circular economy, businesses, governments, and society must work together. But today it is uncertain if reused digital devices may end up being exported illegally and potentially polluting the environment. Such risk and slippage is the main drawback in the promotion and the practice of reuse in public and private organisations.

Therefore, there are three principles that are key to achieving Circular Economy of electronics: Reduce, Reuse and Recycling. we should ensure at the time of recycling devices have low use value, so there is no premature recycling.

5.3 How to measure the circular economy of electronics?

The basic concept of a circular economy depicts a production and consumption system that relies on the recycling, re-use, repair, remanufacturing, sharing of products, changing the consumption patterns and new business models and systems.2

We need to know what works, what doesn’t and why and that means turning to metrics. However, it is at this point that the comparison with history stops. The innovation towards circular business models will require different measurements and metrics than the ones that we developed to monitor and innovate the linear economy.

Metrics that can be used to measure your transition towards a circular business model are often confused with sustainability metrics. If you regard sustainability as the end goal, circular economy is a means towards achieving that goal. Therefore, it is very relevant for business to not only measure its performance in the transition to a circular economy but to also adopt sustainability metrics to measure the impact it is hoping to have by doing so. The field of sustainability science, and the metrics related to that field, is much more mature and has produced many standardized methodologies and metrics such as Life Cycle Analysis (LCA) and reporting on the SDGs. 5.4 Diferences between a metric, indicator, tool, standard or certificate?

FROM Verstraeten-Jochemsen, Baars, and Daniels (2020)

The field of metrics can be difficult to navigate due to the variety of terms and definitions used. What is the difference between a metric and an indicator, and is a standard or a certificate more suitable for your needs? Here’s what we mean with the various terms that we use in our search for the ideal metric for your business:

5.4.1 METRIC

A metric is a method we employ to understand change over time across a number of dimensions. We use it as a catch-all term to describe the method used to measure something, the resulting values, as well as a calculated or combined set of indicators.

5.4.2 INDICATOR

The indicator is a crucial element of a metric, referring to a single value and its unit, and used to indicate (hence the name) a specific trend or performance.

5.4.3 TOOL

Many of the metrics considered are partly or fully automated in online or offline tools that enable easy application and reduce the risk of errors. In the context of this publication, tools can therefore be considered automated metrics.

5.4.4 STANDARD

Metrics that have gone a certain degree of scrutiny and testing, and are commonly accepted as a standardised way of measuring, can be regarded as standards. Technically, one can consider both ‘informal’ standards and standards that have officially been documented and published by standardisation organizations but in the context of this publication, we will only use the term to refer to the latter.

5.4.5 CERTIFICATE

A certificate is a specific type of standard that can not be applied by the organisation itself but requires a third party to assess whether the company’s performance has been according to a certain standard. They usually result in a product label being issued by the third party.

5.5 Challenges

5.5.1 Quality uncertainty

That problem is called “The Market for Lemons: Quality Uncertainty and the Market Mechanism” (AKERLOF 1978) and it refers to issues that arise regarding the value of an investment or product due to asymmetric information possessed by the buyer and the seller. In use case the seller/donor (product owner or the refurbisher) has a product for which it wants to be able to transparently demonstrate the quality to the buyer. In order to minimise the problem the actions to be taken include trying to reduce information asymmetries and be able to stimulate demand and this involves generating sufficient confidence in the second-hand buyer about the quality of the devices before refurishement (Product owner to refurbisher) and after (refurbisher to final user).

Figure 5.3: Circular Economy Challenge

In the case where the seller is a refurbisher and the buyer is an end user there are certain categories that are more valued than others. Mugge, Jockin, and Bocken (2017) investigated the potential of selling refurbished smartphones through a quantitative study. It conducted an online survey questioning 250 respondents to study the impact generated from various incentives that reuse centres can undertake to improve the purchase intention of refurbished phones. Or to put it another way: the question is: how can the reuse market be stimulated? The results are given for 6 different user groups in Figure 5.3. The highest perceived incentive is a renewed (replaced) battery, followed by an assurance that software updates can be performed and that the performance of the item has been improved. In turn, this confirms the barriers or limitations to second-hand purchase in the case of mobiles: a low-performance battery, limited software support and general performance problems. The author comments that it is much easier to convince the “sustainability enthusiast,” as product-related incentives are less important. It is fair to say that “sustainability enthusiasts” are intrinsically motivated to make use of reuse options. For the “proud advanced user,” information and product incentives are much more important. This survey also shows that information about the refurbishment process, a rating system and quality certification are very important in attracting refurbished smartphones.

Els dispositius descartats per administracions públiques o privades, o altres, poden preparar-se o reacondicionar-se, o poden reciclar-se si s’ha detectat que no existeix demanda. Ambdós tenen costos i implicacions diferents i les opcions són clau per comptar amb un marge econòmic operatiu. El primer dels reptes és que els centres de reutilització i reciclatge puguin incentivar als donants de tipu empresa i administració pública per captar els dispositius amortitzats, tant siguin per reutilitzar o reciclar. A continuació presentem un recull d’articles que han fet énfasis en la conveniència de retroalimentar als donants amb informació sobre l’esborrat de dades i l’ impacte generat. Els consumidors estan disposats a donar els seus dispositius? De la primera, Wilson,2017) 4 , tal i com es pot veure a la figura 2, conclou que el 50% dels estudiants que es canviaven el mòbil per un de nou conservaven l’antic. En un article recent, Poppelaars, 2020 6 ., ha revisat les experiències dels usuaris de programes comercials de recollida de telèfons mòbils i proporcionat recomanacions sobre com millorar-les. Conclouque si es facilitava el procediment de devolució amb més orientació i retroalimentació es podria millorar significativament l’experiència de l’usuari dels programes de recollida. Atès que els dispositius es mantenen en calaixos per diversos motius (per exemple, seguretat de les dades, solució de còpia de seguretat, entre d’altres.), els programes de recollida han de proposar alternatives interessants perquè els usuaris puguin mitigar els riscos esperats. Les incerteses sobre qüestions de privadesa i pèrdua de dades es poden reduir mostrant a l’usuari pas a pas com fer la còpia de seguretat i la transferència i l’eliminació de dades quan es prepara el telèfon per al seu enviament. Es podria proporcionar a l’usuari un certificat d’eliminació de dades. A més, per a generar una sensació de satisfacció al final dels processos de liquidació, és clau proporcionar una confirmació al consumidor a la recepció segura del dispositiu. A tall d’exemple, es poden comunicar alguns resultats confirmant la contribució ambiental positiva.

5.5.3 Preserve business privacy

The problem we have is how reconciling business privacy with being able to measure social and environmental impact. When companies, governments or individuals need to get rid of their computers and digital devices to have a new life (reuse), they do not know how to identify which ITADs/Refurbishers can promote a more circular economy. The Refurbishers / Retailers / ITADs / Trade-in actors of the reverse-supply chain do not feel comfortable in openly sharing this traceability and suppliers data. All of them are in competition, or they might even be penalized, if one of their devices is not properly recycled at the end of its life. This results in most Electric and Electronic Equipment (EEE) being recycled even though the explicit demand for reuse.

Figure 5.4: Impact accounting without affecting business privacy

The challenge is to give confidence to donors/sellers that their products will be managed in accordance with the principles of the circular economy, and to assure the actors in the reverse supply chain that their contacts will not be exposed and that they will not be penalised if there is no record of the final recycling of what they have managed. For example how can we know the extended durability of a device and whether it has been properly recycled without having to know the actors involved in the reverse-supply chain and who are the final users. To minimise the problem, on the one hand, the devices, actors and end-users must be completely anonymous and, on the other hand, there must be no possibility of discovery, and on the other hand the impact accounting must provide guarantees of trust, verifiability, irreversibility and tamper proof to ensure sellers/donors that positive impact is generated if they divert their devices to reuse streams.

5.5.3.1 Increase the culture of reuse

The real paradigm shift will occur when ordinary citizens join the second-hand market. On the one hand, it is necessary to encourage collection; to promote that users who dispose of devices do so in local reuse centres that repair, improve and distribute quality second-hand products. On the other hand, it is necessary to encourage demand, so that everyone can consider second-hand consumption, regardless of their economic situation. The latter will be possible if second-hand devices have a long durability that could make consumers resent them, so it should be as repairable (replacement of worn components), upgradeable (adding new components) and upgradable (they can work with current operating systems and applications) as possible. This aspect is related to the first and second problem. We will be able to create a second-hand culture if we know how to generate trust in second-hand quality and impact accounting.
